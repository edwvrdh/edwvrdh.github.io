Introduction

Active Garden is a networked technology consisting of a mobile application and an automatic watering system that encourages the elderly to keep fit and maintain their plants. Users complete at-home workouts and are rewarded with their plants being watered. Workouts are customisable, and plants are registered within the app, where the correct measure of water is inputted for each individual plant.


The aim of this project was to design a product that promotes the concept of active aging to the elderly through networked technologies. In doing so, such individuals maintain positive health, participation, and security in their daily life, which is integral to leading a fulfilling and enjoyable lifestyle in elderhood. Within the scope of this project, it was required that a combination of two networked technologies were to be used to address the problem statement. Only one device/user interface was required to be focused on when prototyping, with the general concept of how it will communicate and connect with the second device being mandatory to be explored.


With a team of four members, I participated in conducting interviews to gather user research, creating a storyboard, a paper prototype and a high-fidelity prototype of the design idea, as well as guiding a participant through user testing evaluation.

Key Interview Findings


Key Insight #1

Concerns for long-term health

Participants acknowledged the degradation of their health as a result of aging, primarily outlining physical challenges such as decreased mobility.




Key Insight #2

Gardening is a popular hobby

Gardening helps with improving the mental and physical health of elderly people. Those that did not have their own garden often stated they would like to do so in the near future.




Key Insight #3

Elderly people like to participate in social groups

Elderly people often participate in social or community groups that relate to their hobbies.




With this user research, it was decided that the proposed technology for active aging would revolve around gardening due to its involvement in the majority of elderly lives, as well as incorporating physical activity to tackle long-term health concerns.




Storyboarding



To better visualise and understand how the proposed technology will be used in the context of an eldery person's home, I created a storyboard that depicts a typical interaction with Active Garden. By visually sharing a hypothetical situation of the target audience, the team was able to share a common understanding of how the technology would function and its design directions.




Prototyping and Testing

With the target audience being elderly people, it was essential that the technology was designed in a manner that was easy to learn. With this in mind, many of the elements within the prototypes are consistent with the user interfaces of many other applications, with a specific focus on utilising icons and visual cues to interact with the app.

Heuristic Evaluation

Various usability testing was conducted to obtain feedback and improve upon each iteration of the technology's prototypes. Such testing includes heuristic evaluation, where the participant was given the task of completing a workout and watering their plants using Active Garden. Information that was recorded includes usability assessment (positive/ negative findings), identification of heuristics (Complied/ Violated heuristic), usability impact (Small, Medium, High) and usability suggestion(s).

Usability Assessment

  • Was able to very easily identify what exercise they were about to start with.
  • Did not like the lack of information regarding the amount of exercises, how long it would take etc.

Identification of Heuristics

  • Error prevention (Complied)
  • Visibility of system status and informative feedback (Violated)

Explanation of Heuristics

  • Provides error prevention through the start button. This makes sure that users who accidentally entered the screen can return to the previous menu without starting a new workout.
  • Does not provide enough information or guidance to the user about what is about to occur.

Usability Impact

  • Medium impact as it prevents any frustrating experiences that can occur when a user accidentally enters this screen
  • The lack of information within this screen means that users are left in the dark about what is and what will occur. Medium impact as this leads to a more challenging experience

Usability Suggestions

  • Providing simple but essential information such as how long the workout will take, how many exercises there are, and the types of exercises they are expected to do. This can be displayed under the “Start” button. This will improve the visibility of system status and informative feedback on the screen.

Usability Assessment

  • Liked the quick and simple statistics with big and bold numbers.
  • Did not like that the “Workout Complete” was so small.

Identification of Heuristics

  • Flexibility and efficiency of use (Complied)
  • Aesthetic and minimalist design (Violated)

Explanation of Heuristics

  • The persistent button at the bottom of the screen provides a method to progress to the next screen, as it remains on the screen no matter how far the user scrolls up or down the page.
  • The “Streak” component is the largest element in the screen but is not the most important.

Usability Impact

  • High impact as it provides a quick and seamless experience for all users as they always have access to the “Water Your Plants” button and therefore benefits their goal to finish their task.
  • The large “Streak” component undermines the “Workout Complete” text that informs the user that they have completed their task. High impact as this causes an unnecessarily hurdle to the user’s ease of use.

Usability Suggestions

  • The “Workout Complete” component should be much larger so that users are instantly notified that they have completed their workout. The “Streak” component can be substantially smaller to accommodate this and can even be changed to include the “Stats” row since this element is not a very major and demanding component of the screen.

High-Fidelity Prototype

A high-fidelity prototype was constructed in Figma using the feedback and recommendations for improvement collected from various usability testing.


Using the "Start Workout" and "Workout Complete" screens shown in the paper prototype section, suggestions to improve usability were incorporated. In addition to this, colours were deliberately selected to heavily contrast from each other, with high visibility aiding the target audience of elderly people to quickly recognise buttons to move to the next screen.





The workout button in the "Home" screen was made more prominent to ensure that it is one of the first elements that is viewed by the user (Left), and a reset button was added to the "Workout" screen to allow users the control and freedom to restart the exercise (Right). Accompanying this is a pause and fast forward button, icons that match real-world items and are therefore easily recognisable.




Conclusion

Active Garden was constructed as a combination of two networked technologies: a mobile app and an automatic watering system. It targets the concept of active aging by encouraging elderly people to improve their physical and mental health, as well as promoting social participation and conversation with their peers through Active Garden’s “Friends” screen, where daily streaks and progress can be compared. By involving gardening, a popular hobby among the elderly, the target audience is provided more incentive to live a healthier lifestyle. Essentially, this project enhanced my understanding of user research and usability testing, and how to utilise feedback from participants to improve prototypes. As quoted by stakeholders, I pertained “Good follow up from the user feedback” when creating the final prototype.
